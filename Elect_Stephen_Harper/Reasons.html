Some Reasons You Might Want to Reconsider Your Harper Vote

  1. His economic record is shoddy, at best. Some of the low points include:

    1. Having the lowest average annual GDP growth in 66 years

    2. Having "the slowest job creation of any Prime Ministers since World War II"

    3. Being the first PM in 60 years to see a decline in the employment rate (PressProgress, 2015)

    4. Relentlessly pursuing a "balanced budget" by cutting stimulus and social programs essential to getting Canada out of a recession while at the same time finding money for always-popular tax credits (Rochon, 2015)

    5. Selling out the Canadian Wheat Board to a Saudi-owned grain company, over the lawsuits and objections of farmers. (Puxley, 2015)


  3. When first elected, instead of continuing the previous Liberal government's attempt to create a publicly-funded childcare program, Harper created child care benefits for children under 6 (later extended to children under 17). However, according to the Parliamentary Budget officer, over half of all families set to receive payments are not actually currently paying for childcare at all. (Rennie, 2015) Some would say this unfairly benefits the wealthy.


  1. Harper has prorogued parliament to avoid political difficulties three times: in December 2008, only six weeks after the last election, to prevent the formation of a Liberal-NDP-BQ coalition which would have toppled his then-minority government with a non-confidence vote (Heard, 2008); in December of 2009, ostensibly for the 2010 Winter Olympics, to prevent a parliamentary inquiry into the abuse of Afghan detainees (LeBlanc, 2012); and in September of 2013 to avoid the Senate expenses scandal. This is a record for the most prorogue time by any Canadian Prime Minister, making a total of 181 days outside of usual holidays and breaks (Chase, 2013).


  1. Under Harper's tenure, the entire Conservative government was found to be in contempt of Parliament, a Commonwealth first, for failing to turn over certain documents related to the Afghan detainee scandal. (CBC News, 2011) While other cases of contempt do exist, they are very rare and usually involve individuals, not whole national government parties. (CanWest News Service, 2008)


  1. Harper has severely restricted press access to the Canadian Ministry. Most notably, his staff get to choose who speaks when and what questions they are and aren't allowed to ask. (Global National, 2006)


  1. Harper's cabinet has chosen, in many official Government of Canada notices, to brand as the "Harper Government" rather than the formal "Her Majesty's Government" or even the minimum "Government of Canada" required by the Federal Identity Program (Cheadle, 2012).


  1. Under Canadian law, all Members of Parliament are members of the government, as they make up the legislative branch. However, Harper has a history of breaking with tradition and treating the executive branch (i.e. himself and his Cabinet) as the only thing making up the "government", as shown by his exclusion of opposition MPs at foreign delegations and local events. (Bell, 2011)


  1. Harper once said that the NDP "did not even want to support war against Hitler" during a debate about the Iraq War. He was wrong on two fronts: the NDP didn't exist in 1939 (he was referring to one of their predecessor parties, the Co-operative Commonwealth Federation), and every party member of the CCF but one voted in favour of the war. (O'Malley, 2012)

  2. Harper spent $833 million in taxpayer money for security at the G8 and G20 summits in Ontario in 2010, which lasted 3 days. By comparison, security for the entire 2010 Olympic Games in Vancouver, which lasted 16 days, was only $898 million, and the previous G8 in London cost only $30 million (Sufrin, 2010). Among other threats, this money was (according to official reports) spent on protecting leaders from "embarrassment to the Canadian government" (Pugliese, 2012).


  1. The Conservatives tried to illegally remove a ballot box to cancel the votes of over 700 Ontario students in the 2011 election. (CTV News video, 2011)

  2. In 1997, Harper said that "In terms of the unemployed, of which we have over a million-and-a-half, I don't feel particularly bad for many of these people." (CBC News, 2006)


  1. Harper has attacked women's rights with comments ranging from saying that "Both men and women taxpayers will pay additional money ... the federal government should scrap its ridiculous pay equity law" and that "'equal pay for work of equal value' ... is not the same as equal pay for the same job." (Hansard, 2006)


  1. Harper allegedly knew about an alleged scheme in which the Conservative party tried to bribe an MP dying of cancer with a million dollar life insurance policy to gain his support in a crucial vote in 2005. (CBC News, 2008)


  1. Harper proposed and passed Bill C-24, which increases the difficulty for immigrants wishing to obtain Canadian citizenship (including removing the ability for would-be citizens to appeal to a judge) and allows Citizenship and Immigration Canada to remove any Canadian's citizenship at any time without the need for a judge or even a face-to-face meeting with the former citizen. (The Georgia Strait, 2014)


  1. Despite campaigning for Senate reform, and introducing a bill to make the Senate elected in 2006, Harper has appointed 57 Senators, the vast majority of whom were Conservative donors. (Roll Up the Red Carpet, 2014)


  1. The Conservative federal government has spent $700 000 fighting wounded Afghanistan veterans in court over the Veteran's Charter, which is allegedly less fair to veterans than the previous pension system. (Brewster, 2015) Despite claiming that the court case is "against the previous Liberal policy", and that his party had nothing to do with the reforms, the Veteran's Charter was in fact implemented and fully supported by the Conservatives back in 2006, with Stephen Harper saying that "Our troops' commitment and service to Canada entitles them to the very best treatment possible. This charter is but a first step towards according Canadian veterans the respect and support they deserve." (Brewster, 2014)
